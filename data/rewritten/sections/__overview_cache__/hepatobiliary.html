Detailed Risk Factors

Hepatitis B Virus (HBV) Chronic Infection

Type: Infectious / Viral

Strength: Strong / Established. Associated with 5-100 fold increased risk.

Detail: Leading cause of HCC globally, especially in Asia and Africa. Causes cirrhosis and also promotes oncogenesis via direct insertional mutagenesis and viral oncoproteins (HBx). The risk is present even in the absence of cirrhosis.

Hepatitis C Virus (HCV) Chronic Infection

Type: Infectious / Viral

Strength: Strong / Established. Associated with 15-20 fold increased risk in cirrhotics.

Detail: The leading cause of HCC in the United States and Europe. Risk is mediated primarily through progressive hepatic fibrosis and cirrhosis. Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) that cure HCV reduce but do not eliminate HCC risk.

Alcoholic Liver Disease / Cirrhosis

Type: Toxic / Metabolic

Strength: Strong / Established. Cirrhosis from any etiology confers a 1-2% annual risk of developing HCC.

Detail: Chronic excessive alcohol consumption is a major cause of cirrhosis, which is the strongest single risk factor for HCC. The combination of alcohol with viral hepatitis synergistically increases risk.

Non-alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD) / Non-alcoholic Steatohepatitis (NASH)

Type: Metabolic

Strength: Strong / Established. Risk is increasing in parallel with the obesity epidemic.

Detail: The fastest-growing cause of HCC in Western nations, often arising in the setting of metabolic syndrome (obesity, type 2 diabetes, dyslipidemia). Can develop HCC in the absence of advanced fibrosis or cirrhosis.

Aflatoxin B1 Exposure

Type: Environmental / Dietary

Strength: Strong / Established. High exposure areas (sub-Saharan Africa, China) have high HCC incidence.

Detail: A potent hepatocarcinogen produced by Aspergillus fungi, contaminating staple grains (corn, peanuts) in tropical climates. Causes a specific A:T→G:C mutation in the TP53 gene (codon 249). Risk is synergistic with HBV infection.

Cirrhosis (from Any Cause)

Type: Pathophysiological State

Strength: Strong / Established. HCC surveillance is recommended for this population.

Detail: The single most important risk factor for HCC. Approximately 80-90% of HCC cases arise in a cirrhotic liver. The annual HCC risk in cirrhotic patients ranges from 1-8%, varying by etiology and severity.

Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC)

Type: Autoimmune / Inflammatory

Strength: Strong / Established for pCCA.

Detail: A chronic cholestatic liver disease strongly associated with inflammatory bowel disease (ulcerative colitis). It is the major risk factor for perihilar cholangiocarcinoma (pCCA). Patients with PSC have a lifetime risk of developing pCCA of 10-15%.

Gallstones (Cholelithiasis)

Type: Inflammatory / Mechanical

Strength: Strong / Established. Gallbladder cancer risk is highest in populations with high prevalence of gallstones.

Detail: The primary risk factor for gallbladder cancer, particularly large stones (>3 cm), which cause chronic inflammation and irritation of the gallbladder mucosa.

Liver Fluke Infections (Opisthorchis viverrini, Clonorchis sinensis)

Type: Infectious / Parasitic

Strength: Strong / Established. Designated as a Group 1 carcinogen by IARC.

Detail: Chronic infection, common in Southeast Asia, causes chronic biliary inflammation and is a major risk factor for intrahepatic and perihilar cholangiocarcinoma.

Intrahepatic Stones (Hepatolithiasis)

Type: Inflammatory

Strength: Strong / Established.

Detail: Stones within the intrahepatic bile ducts cause chronic biliary obstruction and recurrent cholangitis, significantly increasing the risk of intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma.

Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP)

Type: Hereditary / Genetic

Strength: Strong / Established for ampulla of Vater cancer.

Detail: A syndrome caused by germline mutations in the APC gene. Patients have a markedly increased lifetime risk of ampullary carcinoma, among other gastrointestinal malignancies.

Hereditary Hemochromatosis

Type: Hereditary / Metabolic

Strength: Moderate / Established. The risk is mediated via cirrhosis.

Detail: Iron overload disorder that can lead to cirrhosis and subsequently HCC. The risk is particularly elevated in patients who develop cirrhosis.

Diabetes Mellitus Type 2

Type: Metabolic

Strength: Moderate / Established. Associated with 2-3 fold increased risk of HCC.

Detail: An independent risk factor for HCC, especially in the absence of cirrhosis, though the risk is amplified by co-existing NAFLD/NASH. Also associated with increased risk of BTC.

Molecular Pathogenesis

hepatocellular_carcinoma

The molecular pathogenesis of HCC is complex and typically involves multistep carcinogenesis within the context of chronic liver disease. Key genomic events include mutations or alterations in the TERT promoter (found in ~60% of HCC), which confers a survival advantage to hepatocytes. The Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathway is frequently dysregulated, with activating mutations in CTNNB1 (β-catenin) occurring in ~30% of tumors. Other commonly mutated genes include TP53 (associated with aflatoxin B1 exposure and HBV infection), AXIN1, ARID1A, and NFE2L2. Chromosomal instability, including losses at 1p, 4q, 8p, 13q, and 16q, and gains at 1q and 8q, is common. Activation of the PI3K/AKT/mTOR and RAS/RAF/MEK/ERK pathways are critical drivers of proliferation and survival. The integration of viral DNA (HBV) into the host genome and the chronic inflammatory milieu (via cytokines like IL-6) create a pro-carcinogenic environment. [9]

biliary_tract_cancers

Biliary tract cancers exhibit significant molecular heterogeneity based on anatomic subsite. For intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma (iCCA), key driver mutations include IDH1 or IDH2 (in 10-20% of cases), leading to the production of the oncometabolite 2-hydroxyglutarate. FGFR2 gene fusions (e.g., FGFR2-BICC1) are found in approximately 10-15% of iCCA tumors and represent a actionable target. BRAF V600E mutations are present in ~5% of cases. For perihilar and distal cholangiocarcinomas, the molecular profile differs; they more closely resemble pancreatic adenocarcinoma, with a high frequency of KRAS mutations (20-30%), TP53 mutations, and SMAD4 alterations. Overexpression of the HER2 (ERBB2) oncogene is noted in a subset (10-20%) of gallbladder cancers. Chromatin remodeling gene mutations, such as ARID1A, ARID2, and PBRM1, are frequently identified across biliary cancer subtypes. Chronic inflammation is a unifying pathogenic driver, leading to DNA damage and activation of pro-tumorigenic pathways. [10,11]

Management Principles

The NCCN Guidelines for Hepatobiliary Cancers have been separated into the NCCN Guidelines for Hepatocellular Carcinoma and the NCCN Guidelines for Biliary Tract Cancers [1]. This separation acknowledges the distinct biological behaviors, staging systems, and treatment paradigms for these two major categories of hepatobiliary malignancies. The overarching management philosophy for both hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) and biliary tract cancers (BTC) is multidisciplinary, with treatment decisions tailored to the specific disease site, stage, underlying liver function (e.g., cirrhosis, Child-Pugh score), and patient performance status. A core principle is to balance oncologic efficacy with the preservation of liver function, which is often compromised by the underlying liver disease. For localized disease, the intent is curative, often involving locoregional therapies. For advanced or metastatic disease, the intent shifts to palliation, aiming to extend survival and improve quality of life.

Treatment Intent

Curative Intent

Patients with early-stage, resectable or locally advanced HCC or BTC who are fit for surgery or curative locoregional therapy. Aggressive multimodal therapy, typically centered on surgical resection. For HCC, this may include ablation, transarterial chemoembolization (TACE), or transplant within strict criteria. For BTC, it includes adjuvant therapy following resection.

Palliative Intent

Patients with advanced, metastatic, or unresectable HCC or BTC, or those with significant comorbidities limiting aggressive treatment. Systemic therapy, with options guided by biomarkers (e.g., FGFR2 fusions for BTC, PD-L1 status for some immunotherapies), and supportive care. Locoregional therapies like TACE or radiation may be used for symptom control or to treat oligometastatic disease.

Systemic Therapy

Systemic therapy is indicated for advanced, metastatic, or unresectable hepatobiliary cancers. Treatment selection depends on the primary tumor site (HCC vs. BTC), underlying liver function, performance status, and biomarker status (e.g., FGFR2 fusions, IDH1 mutations, MSI-H/dMMR for BTC). For HCC, targeted therapy and immunotherapy have become standard. For BTC, platinum-based chemotherapy is first-line, with targeted therapies for specific molecular alterations.

By Stage and Setting:
- HCC First-line: Atezolizumab plus bevacizumab (IMbrave150). Alternatives: sorafenib or lenvatinib.
- HCC Second-line: Regorafenib, cabozantinib, ramucirumab (if AFP ≥400), or pembrolizumab/nivolumab.
- BTC First-line: Gemcitabine plus cisplatin (ABC-02). Alternatives: GEMOX or fluoropyrimidine-based. For MSI-H/dMMR: pembrolizumab.
- BTC Second-line: Fluoropyrimidine-based (FOLFOX, capecitabine). Targeted: futibatinib for FGFR2 fusions, ivosidenib for IDH1 mutations.
- BTC Adjuvant: Capecitabine for 6 months (BILCAP).

Key Regimens:
1. Atezolizumab plus Bevacizumab: First-line advanced HCC. Drugs: Atezolizumab 1200 mg IV Day 1, Bevacizumab 15 mg/kg IV Day 1. 21-day cycle. Toxicity: Hypertension, proteinuria, diarrhea, hypothyroidism, hepatitis, colitis, rash.
2. Sorafenib: First-line alternative or second-line. 400 mg PO BID continuous. Toxicity: Hand-foot skin reaction, diarrhea, hypertension, fatigue.
3. Lenvatinib: First-line alternative. 8 mg (if <60 kg) or 12 mg (if ≥60 kg) PO daily. Toxicity: Hypertension, diarrhea, decreased appetite, weight loss, proteinuria.
4. Gemcitabine plus Cisplatin: First-line advanced BTC. Gemcitabine 1000 mg/m2 IV Days 1&8, Cisplatin 25 mg/m2 IV Days 1&8. 21-day cycle. Toxicity: Myelosuppression, nausea, vomiting, fatigue, nephrotoxicity.
5. Capecitabine (Adjuvant BTC): 1250 mg/m2 PO BID Days 1-14, 21-day cycle x 8 cycles. Toxicity: Hand-foot syndrome, diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, myelosuppression.
6. Futibatinib: Previously treated ICC with FGFR2 fusion. 20 mg PO daily continuous. Toxicity: Hyperphosphatemia, stomatitis, dry eye, fatigue, diarrhea.

Radiation Therapy

Radiation therapy (RT) plays a limited but important role in hepatobiliary cancers, primarily for palliation of symptoms (e.g., pain, biliary obstruction), treatment of oligometastatic disease, or as adjuvant therapy for positive surgical margins. Its use is often constrained by the radiosensitivity of the surrounding liver parenchyma and gastrointestinal structures.

Principles

  • Treatment planning must spare the uninvolved liver to prevent radiation-induced liver disease (RILD).
  • Conformal techniques (3D-CRT, IMRT, SBRT) are essential to deliver therapeutic doses while minimizing toxicity.
  • The tolerance dose of the whole liver is approximately 30-35 Gy; focal treatments can deliver higher doses to limited volumes.
  • For BTC, RT is often combined with concurrent chemotherapy (e.g., fluoropyrimidines) as a radiosensitizer.

Treatment Approaches

Adjuvant Radiation for BTC after R1/R2 Resection

Dose/Fractionation: 45-54 Gy in 1.8-2.0 Gy fractions

Concurrent Chemotherapy: Capecitabine or 5-FU

Indication: Positive surgical margins after resection of BTC (extrahepatic CCA, gallbladder cancer).

Key Trial: Data are retrospective; the BILCAP trial established adjuvant capecitabine, but the role of RT is less defined. Some centers use RT for margin-positive disease.

Toxicities: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, fatigue, liver toxicity (rare with modern techniques).

SBRT for Oligometastatic Liver Disease

Dose/Fractionation: 30-50 Gy in 3-5 fractions

Concurrent Chemotherapy: None typically

Indication: Limited number of liver metastases (≤3-5) from BTC or HCC, not amenable to resection or ablation.

Key Trial: Multiple phase II trials support SBRT for liver metastases; no large randomized trial specific for BTC.

Toxicities: Liver toxicity (RILD), gastrointestinal toxicity if adjacent to stomach/bowel, fatigue.

Palliative Radiation for Bone Metastases

Dose/Fractionation: 8 Gy in 1 fraction or 30 Gy in 10 fractions

Concurrent Chemotherapy: None

Indication: Painful bone metastases from HCC or BTC.

Key Trial: Standard palliative RT principles apply.

Toxicities: Fatigue, local pain flare, rare myelosuppression.

Surgery

Surgical resection is the only potentially curative treatment for localized hepatobiliary cancers (HCC, BTC) when feasible. It offers the best chance for long-term survival.

Principles:
1. The goal is complete resection with negative microscopic margins (R0 resection).
2. Surgical planning must account for the underlying liver disease (e.g., cirrhosis) to prevent post-hepatectomy liver failure.
3. The extent of resection is determined by tumor location, number, and liver functional reserve.
4. Regional lymphadenectomy is recommended for BTC to adequately stage the disease, though its therapeutic benefit is debated.
5. Surgery should be performed at high-volume centers by experienced hepatobiliary surgeons to minimize complications and mortality.

For HCC: Resectable if tumor is technically removable, patient has adequate liver function (Child-Pugh A or selected B), sufficient future liver remnant (typically >40% of total liver volume in normal liver, >50-60% in cirrhotic liver), no uncontrolled portal hypertension (platelet count >100,000, no varices), and no extrahepatic metastases. For BTC: Resectable if no distant metastases, no involvement of major vascular structures (e.g., main portal vein, hepatic artery) that would preclude R0 resection, and adequate biliary drainage can be achieved. Unresectable disease is defined by extensive vascular invasion, multifocal disease beyond resectable limits, distant metastases, or insufficient liver reserve.

Optimization of liver function is critical, especially in cirrhotic patients. This includes management of ascites, nutritional support, and possibly portal vein embolization (PVE) to induce hypertrophy of the future liver remnant if it is small. Biliary drainage (e.g., via ERCP or PTC) may be necessary in patients with obstructive jaundice to reduce bilirubin levels and improve liver function preoperatively, particularly for perihilar CCA. Correction of coagulopathy and anemia is also important.

Surgical Procedures

Hepatic Resection (Hemihepatectomy, Segmentectomy, Wedge Resection)

Approach: Open or minimally invasive (laparoscopic/robotic).

Indications: Localized HCC or ICC with sufficient functional liver reserve (typically Child-Pugh A, no portal hypertension).

Key Complications: Post-hepatectomy liver failure, bile leak, hemorrhage, infection.

Operative Mortality: Varies by center and extent of resection; generally <5% at high-volume centers for minor resections, but can be higher for major hepatectomies in cirrhotic patients.

Pancreaticoduodenectomy (Whipple Procedure)

Approach: Open or minimally invasive.

Indications: Resectable distal cholangiocarcinoma, ampullary carcinoma, or pancreatic head carcinoma.

Key Complications: Delayed gastric emptying, pancreatic fistula, biliary leak, hemorrhage, infection.

Operative Mortality: Typically 2-5% at high-volume centers.

Radical Cholecystectomy

Approach: Open or laparoscopic converted to open.

Indications: Gallbladder cancer ≥T1b or with suspected lymph node involvement.

Key Complications: Similar to hepatic resection and bile duct injury.

Operative Mortality: Low when performed as a planned procedure, higher if incidental cancer is discovered and requires reoperation.

Bile Duct Resection and Reconstruction

Approach: Open.

Indications: Perihilar cholangiocarcinoma (Klatskin tumor) or distal CCA requiring bile duct excision.

Key Complications: Bile leak, stricture, cholangitis.

Operative Mortality: Higher than simple cholecystectomy; risk increases with extent of resection.
